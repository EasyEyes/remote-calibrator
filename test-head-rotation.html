IPD Correction Geometry

The Problem

EasyEyes estimates viewing distance from the interpupillary distance (IPD) — the pixel distance between the two eye centers as seen by the webcam. When the observer faces the camera straight on, the full physical IPD projects onto the image plane and the distance estimate is accurate.

When the observer turns their head (yaw), the line connecting the two eyes is no longer perpendicular to the camera's line of sight. The 2D projection of the IPD shrinks by \(\cos(\psi)\), where \(\psi\) is the yaw angle. This makes the eyes appear closer together, causing the system to overestimate viewing distance.

We correct for this by estimating the yaw angle from the face mesh and dividing out the shrinkage factor.

  • L, R — left and right eye positions in 3D.
  • M — midpoint between the two eyes.
  • d — viewing distance from camera to eye midpoint.
  • \(\psi\) — yaw angle (rotation about the vertical axis).

Apparent vs. True IPD

When the head is rotated by yaw angle \(\psi\):

$$\text{IPD}_{\text{apparent}} = \text{IPD}_{\text{true}} \cdot \cos(\psi)$$

The camera sees only the horizontal projection of the interocular segment. At \(\psi = 0\) (facing straight), the full IPD is visible. At \(\psi = 30°\), only \(\cos(30°) \approx 86.6\%\) of the true IPD is visible.

The Shrinkage Factor

We define:

$$\text{ipdShrinkage} = \cos(\psi)$$

And correct the measured IPD ratio by dividing out the shrinkage:

$$\texttt{ipdOverWidth}_{\text{corrected}} = \frac{\texttt{ipdOverWidth}_{\text{measured}}}{\text{ipdShrinkage}}$$

This restores the value that would have been measured if the observer were facing the camera straight on. The corrected ratio is then used in the standard viewing distance formula.

Estimating Yaw from FaceMesh (No Calibration)

We estimate the yaw angle directly from the 2D FaceMesh landmarks, without requiring calibration or the unreliable Z coordinate.

The Nose-Tip Trick

The nose protrudes along the face-forward axis. This means its 2D image position encodes which way the head is pointing: when the head turns, the nose tip shifts along the interocular axis relative to the eye midpoint, while a forward-facing head has no such lateral shift.

Note that "lateral" here means along the interocular axis — not necessarily vertical in the image. The algorithm works in the head's own reference frame, so the exact image position of the nose relative to the eyes (e.g. whether it appears below or at an angle) doesn't matter. What matters is the component of the nose offset along vs. perpendicular to the eye-to-eye axis. 

  Facing camera          Turned right (yaw > 0)
  ┌─────────┐            ┌─────────┐
  │  L   R  │            │ L   R   │
  │    M    │            │   M     │
  │    |    │            │    \    │
  │    N    │            │     N   │
  └─────────┘            └─────────┘
     zero lateral            nose shifted along
     shift along L→R         the interocular axis

Head-Relative Coordinate Frame

Rather than working in raw image coordinates (which would break if the head is tilted/rolled), we decompose the nose offset in a coordinate frame defined by the interocular axis itself:

  1. Along axis — unit vector from left eye to right eye: $$\hat{a} = \frac{\vec{R} - \vec{L}}{|\vec{R} - \vec{L}|}$$
  2. Perpendicular axis — 90° rotation of the along axis in the image plane, pointing roughly from eyes toward nose (downward when head is upright): $$\hat{p} = \text{rotate}_{90°}(\hat{a})$$ If \(\hat{a} = (a_x,\, a_y)\), then \(\hat{p} = (-a_y,\, a_x)\).

Decomposing the Nose Offset

Let \(\vec{M}\) be the eye midpoint and \(\vec{N}\) the nose tip. The offset vector \(\vec{d} = \vec{N} - \vec{M}\) is decomposed as:

$$\text{lateralShift} = \vec{d} \cdot \hat{a}$$ $$\text{forwardProjection} = \vec{d} \cdot \hat{p}$$
  • lateralShift — how far the nose has shifted along the interocular axis. This is zero when facing the camera and grows with yaw.
  • forwardProjection — how far the nose projects perpendicular to the interocular axis. This is the "depth" of the nose as seen in the image.

3D Interpretation

In 3D, the nose tip sits at a fixed distance \(L\) (the nose protrusion length) in front of the eye midpoint, along the face-forward direction. When the head rotates by yaw \(\psi\) and pitch \(\phi\):

$$\text{lateralShift} \approx L \cdot \sin(\psi)$$ $$\text{forwardProjection} \approx L \cdot \cos(\psi) \cdot \cos(\phi)$$

The nose protrusion \(L\) cancels out in the ratio, making the estimate scale-invariant — it works identically whether the FaceMesh provides normalized \([0, 1]\) coordinates or pixel-scale coordinates.

Computing Yaw

From the two components:

$$\psi = \text{atan2}(\text{lateralShift},\; \text{forwardProjection})$$

Using atan2 (rather than asin or atan) gives robust results across the full angular range and avoids division-by-zero issues.

Safety clamp: The forwardProjection denominator is clamped to a minimum of \(0.15 \times \text{IPD}_{norm}\) to prevent instability at extreme angles (e.g. when the face is nearly in profile and the nose tip overlaps the eye midpoint).

Computing Pitch

Pitch (up/down head nod) is derived from the same decomposition:

$$\cos(\phi) \approx \frac{\text{forwardProjection}}{L_{\text{est}} \cdot \cos(\psi)}$$

where \(L_{\text{est}} = \text{hypot}(\text{lateralShift},\, \text{forwardProjection})\) is the estimated nose protrusion.

The sign of forwardProjection determines pitch direction:

  • Positive → nose below eyes → normal upright or tilting back (positive pitch)
  • Negative → nose above eyes → bowing forward (negative pitch)

Pitch does not affect the IPD correction, but is logged as useful diagnostic data.

Applying the Correction

In Distance Tracking (distanceTrack.js)

Every frame, getMeshData() returns the head orientation alongside the eye positions. The rendering function applies: 

const correctedIpdOverWidth = ipdOverWidthMeasured / ipdShrinkage

before computing viewing distance.

In Distance Calibration (distance.js, spaceKeyHandler.js)

During calibration snapshots, the same correction is applied to the measured IPD before saving the calibrated focal-length ratio.

In Distance Check (checkDistance.js, faceMeshValidation.js)

Each validation snapshot corrects the IPD measurement and logs yawDeg, pitchDeg, and ipdShrinkage into the distance check JSON.

Logged Data

Each measurement snapshot records:

Field Type Description
headYawDeg number Yaw in degrees (positive = turned right)
headPitchDeg number Pitch in degrees (positive = tilted up)
ipdShrinkage number \(\cos(\psi)\), clamped to \([0.5, 1]\)

Limitations and Assumptions

  1. Pitch independence. The correction only accounts for yaw. Pitch reduces the vertical component of the nose offset but does not affect the horizontal IPD projection. At moderate pitch (\(< 20°\)), the \(\cos(\phi)\) factor in forwardProjection is close to 1 and has negligible effect on yaw estimation.

  2. Roll tolerance. By using the interocular axis as the reference frame (rather than the image vertical), the algorithm is inherently tolerant of head roll (tilting ear toward shoulder). The decomposition adapts to the actual eye positions regardless of roll.

  3. Nose protrusion assumption. The algorithm assumes the nose protrudes approximately along the face-forward axis. This is true for the vast majority of face shapes. The protrusion length \(L\) cancels out, so nose size does not matter.

  4. Extreme angles. Beyond approximately \(\pm 60°\) of yaw, the face landmarks become unreliable (the far eye may be occluded). The ipdShrinkage is clamped to a minimum of 0.5 (corresponding to \(60°\)) to prevent overcorrection from noisy data.

  5. Z coordinate not used. FaceMesh provides a Z coordinate for each landmark, but empirical testing showed it is too noisy to improve distance estimation. This algorithm deliberately uses only the 2D (x, y) projections.
